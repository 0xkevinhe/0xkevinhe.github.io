A Brief Report On Bitcoin's Next Upgrade For All

2024-12-01
blockchain

Modified: 2024-12-01

Introduction

Discussions about Bitcoin’s next upgrade have been ongoing, yet as of December 2024, the community remains divided on whether to upgrade, the issues an upgrade should address, and the functionalities it should bring. The debate often resembles a political deadlock, with stakeholders entrenched in their positions.

This stalemate has revealed several intriguing phenomena:

  1. Certain community members often reference specific opcodes, motivated either by information asymmetry or commercial interests.

  2. Some projects rely on opcodes that "might" be introduced.

Considering Bitcoin’s FDV (Fully Diluted Value) is nearing $2 trillion and its influence extends across various societal sectors, its upgrades can no longer be decided by a select group of professionals in a single roundtable meeting.

Equal access to information is essential for meaningful discussions. This article aims to provide readers with critical insights into Bitcoin’s upgrade processes, define what Bitcoin upgrades entail, analyze historical patterns, evaluate current proposals, and present key takeaways. Through this, the article seeks to establish a foundation for further discussions on Bitcoin upgrades. Additionally, the author shares reflections on Bitcoin’s governance mechanisms that arose during the research process.

Upgrade Overview: What and Why

What is a Bitcoin Upgrade?

Bitcoin’s whitepaper (https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf) defines a protocol that operates a blockchain network composed of thousands of nodes following the Bitcoin protocol.

These implementations, or clients, come in various versions, with Bitcoin Core (https://github.com/bitcoin/bitcoin) being the most widely used client, as shown by data from https://bitnodes.io/nodes/.

Thus, the Bitcoin Core maintainers (referred to as Core-Devs or Protocol Developers) have significant influence over Bitcoin’s development.

Bitcoin node software consists of multiple modules, and upgrades are defined through Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs) (https://github.com/bitcoin/bips). When discussing Bitcoin upgrades, it usually refers to consensus protocol upgrades—modifications requiring agreement among the majority of the network’s nodes to avoid forks.

As illustrated below, Bitcoin’s consensus protocol modules and related BIP proposals are of particular interest in upgrade discussions.

According to statistics from the Bitcoin GitHub repository (https://github.com/bitcoin/bitcoin/pulse/monthly), development activity is vibrant. However, most changes are unrelated to the consensus protocol and thus do not attract widespread attention.

Types of Consensus Upgrades

Per BIP-123, consensus protocol upgrades are classified into Soft Forks and Hard Forks:

Feature Soft Fork Hard Fork
Compatibility Compatible with old nodes Incompatible with old nodes
Network-wide Update Not always required Required
Risk of Chain Splits Low High
Examples SegWit/Taproot Bitcoin XT/Bitcoin Cash

Another intuitive way to interpret these is as follows:

  • Soft Fork: Adds or strengthens rules (e.g., introducing new functionalities like Taproot addresses).
  • Hard Fork: Removes or loosens rules (e.g., removing block reward limitations).

Soft Fork Process (BIP Workflows)

The previous two successful consensus upgrades (SegWit and Taproot) both utilized soft forks, avoiding significant community splits. Thus, this article focuses on soft forks, which enable upgrades while maintaining compatibility with older software.

The typical workflow of a BIP proposal leading to a soft fork is illustrated below:

Source: https://river.com/learn/what-is-a-bitcoin-improvement-proposal-bip/

Soft forks often combine multiple BIPs into a single proposal. For example, Taproot incorporated three BIPs:

  1. Schnorr Signature (BIP-340): https://github.com/bitcoin/bips/blob/master/bip-0340.mediawiki
  2. Taproot (BIP-341): https://github.com/bitcoin/bips/blob/master/bip-0341.mediawiki
  3. Tapscript (BIP-342): https://github.com/bitcoin/bips/blob/master/bip-0342.mediawiki

Timeline of the Taproot Upgrade

Key milestones in a typical soft fork:

  1. Corresponding BIPs proposed and reviewed.
  2. Core developers submit a GitHub pull request.
  3. Code is reviewed, merged, and activation methods determined.
  4. Miners signal via on-chain voting to decide activation block height.
  5. The new Bitcoin-core client version was released.
  6. Block height reached, completing the upgrade.

Why Upgrade?

Upgrades involve a lengthy process. Readers may wonder, is upgrading necessary? The table below analyzes the pros and cons of consensus changes.

Consensus Change Pros Cons
Change Technological AdvancementEnhanced SecurityExpanded Use Cases Risk of ForkingIncreased Complexity
Unchanged Stability and TrustAvoids Split RisksMinimizes Attack Surface Technological StagnationLacks Flexibility for New Demands

In general, continuous upgrades, as needed, are preferable from the perspectives of sustainability and adaptability.

In-Depth Analysis of Upgrades

Stakeholders in Bitcoin Upgrades

In Bitcoin’s history, the Hong Kong Consensus (signed during the Bitcoin Roundtable in February 2016, https://medium.com/@bitcoinroundtable/bitcoin-roundtable-consensus-266d475a61ff) identified three primary stakeholder groups:

  1. Core Developers (Core-Devs)
  2. Mining Pools
  3. Users and Ecosystem Developers (primarily exchanges, chip manufacturers, etc.)

As Bitcoin adoption has grown rapidly, the stakeholder landscape has evolved from this simple triad into a more fragmented and competitive environment. This is illustrated in the report from Bitcoin-Cap.

Among these stakeholders, several key roles are worth highlighting:

  1. Economic Nodes: These include major centralized exchanges (CEX), payment processors, and custodial service providers. Their stance on soft forks heavily influences what is considered "legitimate" Bitcoin and impacts adoption rates.
  2. Investors: With Bitcoin ETFs, institutional reserves, and even sovereign reserves becoming mainstream, this group has grown increasingly complex.
  3. Users & Ecosystem Developers: Following the Taproot upgrade, Bitcoin's ecosystem has flourished, giving rise to protocols like Ordinals and numerous native applications and scaling solutions.

Key Observations on Stakeholders:

  1. Different roles at different stages: For instance, Ecosystem Developers actively push proposals, Core-Devs exercise their authority over BIP reviews, while mining pools and economic nodes wield significant influence during activation.
  2. Commercial interests drive proposals: Ecosystem Developers often propose and support upgrades aligned with their business interests. For example, proponents of Validity Rollup are inclined to support OP_CAT.
  3. Protocol Developers (Core-Devs): This group currently tends to veto certain proposals rather than actively championing them.

What Makes a Good Upgrade Proposal?

Based on the analysis above, a good upgrade proposal should:

  1. Uphold Bitcoin’s Core Role as a Payment System: Any upgrade must align with Bitcoin’s unique positioning.
  2. Strike a Balance Between Potential Benefits and Risks: It should be broadly acceptable and not polarizing.
  3. Have an Appropriate Scope: Proposals should neither be too trivial nor overly complex to implement.
  4. Timing: Upgrades should address pressing needs, such as SegWit addressing the scalability demand during its time.

Community Sentiment

From the chart above, the community appears to have reached some consensus on the problems upgrades should address, particularly in two areas:

  1. Programmability: Enhancing UTXO programmability for use cases like covenants, vaults, transaction introspection, conditional payments, and script enhancements (e.g., CTV).
  2. Scalability: Improving Layer 2 scaling, with proposals divided into on-chain and off-chain verification solutions (e.g., CAT).

The Puzzle of Consensus

Bitcoin’s community appears trapped in a "consensus maze" for the next upgrade due to the following reasons:

  1. Too Big to Change: With a fully diluted value (FDV) nearing $2 trillion, stakeholders lean towards stability, as no one wants to bear responsibility for potential mishaps.
  2. Fragmented Stakeholders: Diverse interests and varying levels of influence at different stages make consensus elusive; even governments have emerged as stakeholders.
  3. Lack of Governance Mechanisms: As the first blockchain, Bitcoin lacks on-chain governance systems. The community struggles to agree on soft fork activation methods.
  4. Role of Core-Devs: Core developers are increasingly inclined to veto proposals rather than proactively driving them forward.
  5. Lack of Urgency: With maturing blockchain infrastructure, there’s no overwhelming demand for immediate Bitcoin upgrades.

Conclusion & Takeaways

This article introduced the fundamental concepts of Bitcoin upgrades, provided an in-depth analysis of historical upgrades, and reviewed active proposals for the next upgrade. The causes of the current "consensus puzzle" were also identified.

Key takeaways:

  1. Upgrades are essential, with soft forks being the preferred approach.
  2. Stakeholders are highly fragmented, leading to a conservative stance from the community.
  3. Discussions about upgrades must adhere to Bitcoin’s core value proposition.
  4. Scalability is just one of many focus areas for upgrades.
  5. Timing is critical—strong proposals will gain consensus more quickly.
  6. The community must explore improved governance mechanisms.
